Bio

Two filmographies. One serious relationship with story.

Moira Lang

Commercial instinct, queer spunk and ache, class friction, and years inside Philippine cinema.

Moira Lang is what happens when commercial instinct, queer spunk and ache, class friction, and years inside the workings of Philippine cinema all get locked in a room and decide to make something together.

She has co-written films that made people cry over a nation of mothers separated from their children (Anak), see themselves and their conflicted families (Tanging Yaman), flirt with hunger and longing (Kailangan Kita), and turn OFW heartbreak into aching romance (Milan).

She later produced films that pushed further outward: Ang Pagdadalaga ni Maximo Oliveros, which turned queer awakening into something tender, funny, and politically dangerous; Zombadings 1: Patayin sa Shokot si Remington!, a gleeful exorcism of macho panic and small-town hypocrisy; Norte, Hangganan ng Kasaysayan, a portrait of privilege and desperation in a country where revolutions are betrayed by the very people who inherit them; Patay na si Hesus, which transformed familial chaos into a hilariously profane and deeply Filipino meditation on grief; and Manila's Finest, a fever dream of power, corruption, and a nation sleepwalking toward authoritarianism.

Her filmography moves between the mainstream and the margins without settling comfortably in either. It has started conversations, won awards, offended, and made audiences laugh, sob, and question what they were raised to believe.

Michiko Yamamoto

Emotional precision, distrust of sentimentality, and a deep belief in tenderness.

Michiko Yamamoto writes like she distrusts sentimentality and believes in tenderness.

A mathematics graduate who found her way into cinema through instinct, observation, and emotional precision, she has spent the last two decades writing some of the most quietly devastating films in Philippine cinema.

Her work includes Magnifico, which found grace and dignity in poverty without looking away from its cruelties; Ang Pagdadalaga ni Maximo Oliveros, a tender portrait of queer adolescence in a world already teaching it shame; Zombadings 1: Patayin sa Shokot si Remington!, a gleeful exorcism of macho panic and small-town hypocrisy; and On the Job, Honor Thy Father, and Manila's Finest, portraits of corruption, survival, and institutions that consume the people trapped inside them.

Her filmography moves between intimacy and violence without announcing the shift. She writes ordinary people cornered by systems larger than themselves: families, churches, governments, masculinity, poverty, history. Even at their funniest, her films know how close people are to breaking.

Michiko rarely explains her work in public. The films do the talking. They have won awards, unsettled audiences, and quietly reshaped the emotional vocabulary of contemporary Filipino cinema.
